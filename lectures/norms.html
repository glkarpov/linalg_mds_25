Norms

A norm is a quantitative measure of the magnitude of a vector and is usually denoted as \(\Vert x \Vert\).

A norm must satisfy certain properties:

  1. \(\Vert \alpha x \Vert = \vert \alpha\vert \Vert x \Vert\), \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\)
  2. \(\Vert x + y \Vert \leq \Vert x \Vert + \Vert y \Vert\) (triangle inequality)
  3. If \(\Vert x \Vert = 0\), then \(x = 0\)

. . .

The distance between two vectors is defined as \[ d(x, y) = \Vert x - y \Vert. \] The most widely used norm is the Euclidean norm: \[ \Vert x \Vert_2 = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|^2}, \] which corresponds to distance in our real life. If vectors have complex elements, we use their modulus. The Euclidean norm, or \(2\)-norm, is a subclass of the important class of \(p\)-norms:

\[ \Vert x \Vert_p = \Big(\sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|^p\Big)^{1/p}. \]

\(p\)-norm of a vector

There are two very important special cases. The infinity norm, or Chebyshev norm, is defined as the maximum absolute value of vector elements: \[ \Vert x \Vert_{\infty} = \max_i | x_i| \]

. . .

The \(l_1\) norm (or Manhattan distance) is defined as the sum of absolute values of vector elements \(x\):

\[ \Vert x \Vert_1 = \sum_i |x_i| \]

Dot product

Definition

Definition

The dot product of two vectors \(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n\) is a number computed by the formula:

\[\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} u_i v_i = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2 + \cdots + u_n v_n\]

Geometric meaning: \[\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}| \cdot |\mathbf{v}| \cdot \cos \theta\]

where \(\theta\) is the angle between vectors \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\).

Dot product notation

Different ways of writing

1. Through transpose

\[\mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{v}\]

Matrix form: \[\mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} u_1 & u_2 & \cdots & u_n \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ \vdots \\ v_n \end{bmatrix}\]

Result: \[= u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2 + \cdots + u_n v_n\]

2. Through angle brackets

\[\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle\]

Alternative notation: - \(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}\) ‚Äî dot product

Notations are equivalent: \[\mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{v} = \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}\]

Basic properties of dot product

Algebraic properties

  1. Commutativity: \[\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u} \rangle\]

  2. Linearity in the first argument: \[\langle \alpha \mathbf{u} + \beta \mathbf{w}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \alpha \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle + \beta \langle \mathbf{w}, \mathbf{v} \rangle\]

Geometric properties

  1. Connection with vector length: \[|\mathbf{u}| = \sqrt{\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{u} \rangle} = \sqrt{\mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{u}}\]

  2. Orthogonality: \[\mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{v} \Leftrightarrow \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0\]
